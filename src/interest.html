Topic: Interest

Simple Interest

Let’s begin by looking at simple interest. What does it mean to invest a principal amount of money P in a bank at a simple interest rate r?

The bank’s customers are letting the bank use their money for a period of time, and in return, the bank is willing to return their original amount at the end of the period plus extra money called Interest. The interest earned depends on the rate and the period of time.

For example, if Mrs. Hamlisch puts $200 into a savings account at 12% interest, at the end of the year her account will have $200 plus the interest she earned. This interest is 12% of $200, which equals $24. Thus, at the end of the year, she will have $200 + $24 = $224 in her bank account.

Simple Interest Formula

The formula for calculating simple interest is:

I = Prt

Where:

  • P is the principal amount invested.
  • r is the interest rate (expressed as a decimal).
  • t is the time in years.

The total amount in the account, including interest, is given by:

A = P(1 + rt)

Example 1: Simple Interest Calculation

Mr. Lablanc invests $500 at a simple interest rate of 6%. How much interest will he earn in one month? In six months? In one year?

For the interest in one month, we use the simple interest formula I = Prt, where:

  • P = $500
  • r = 6% or 0.06 annually
  • t = 1 month or 1/12 years

For one month: I = 500 × 0.06 × (1/12) = $2.50

For six months: I = 500 × 0.06 × (6/12) = $15.00

For one year: I = 500 × 0.06 × 1 = $30.00

Compound Interest

Most financial institutions use compound interest, which means the interest is applied after each period (usually monthly or annually). The compound interest formula is:

A = P(1 + i)^n

Where:

  • P is the initial principal.
  • i is the interest rate per period.
  • n is the number of periods.

Example 2: Compound Interest Calculation

Suppose we deposit $1000 at 12% per year compounded monthly. How much will be in the account after six months?

We use the compound interest formula A(n) = P(1 + i)^n. In this case:

  • P = $1000
  • r = 12% or 0.12
  • m = 12 (monthly compounding)
  • n = 6 months

So, i = 0.12 / 12 = 0.01 and n = 6, we get:

A(6) = 1000 × (1 + 0.01)^6 = 1061.52

Therefore, after six months, the amount in the account will be $1061.52.

Formula for the amount at any time t months:

A(t) = 1000(1 + 0.12/12)^t

From the graph of this function, we estimate it will take about 10 months for the account to reach $1,100.
