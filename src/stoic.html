Topic: Stoichiometry

Stoichiometry Formulas

Stoichiometry involves the study of quantitative relationships between reactants and products in chemical reactions. The core areas include:

  • Chemical equations
  • The mole and molar mass
  • Chemical formulas
  • Mass relationships in equations
  • Limiting reactant

Chemical Equations

A chemical equation represents a chemical reaction with reactants, products, and coefficients indicating the number of moles or molecules. The coefficients are balanced according to the Law of Conservation of Mass, ensuring the mass of reactants equals the mass of products.

Writing Balanced Chemical Equations

To balance a chemical equation, follow these steps:

  1. Write the unbalanced equation.
  2. Count the atoms of each element on both sides.
  3. Adjust the coefficients to balance the equation.
  4. Double-check the coefficients and atom count.
  5. Simplify if necessary by using the lowest common multiple.

The Mole and Molar Mass

A mole represents a specific quantity of a substance: approximately 6.022 × 10²³ atoms or molecules. The molar mass is the mass of one mole of a substance, calculated by summing the atomic weights of all elements in its chemical formula. For example, the molar mass of water (H2O) is 18.02 g/mol (2 × 1.01 + 16.00).

Chemical Formulas

Chemical formulas represent the composition of a compound using chemical symbols and subscripts. For example, table salt (NaCl) consists of one sodium atom and one chlorine atom.

Examples of chemical formulas include:

  • Water: H2O
  • Carbon dioxide: CO2
  • Sodium chloride: NaCl

Mass Relationships in Equations

Mass relationships in stoichiometry involve calculating the masses of reactants and products using atomic and molecular masses:

  • Atomic Mass: The average mass of an atom of an element (e.g., Carbon's atomic mass is 12.01 g/mol).
  • Molecular Mass: The mass of a molecule, calculated by summing the atomic masses of the atoms within the molecule.
  • Formula Mass: The sum of the atomic masses of all atoms in a chemical formula, useful for ionic compounds.

Percent Composition

Percent composition expresses the relative amounts of each element in a compound. It is calculated by dividing the mass of each element by the total mass of the compound and multiplying by 100%. For example, the percent composition of water (H2O) is:

  • Hydrogen: 11.19%
  • Oxygen: 88.81%

Limiting Reagent/Reactant

The limiting reagent is the reactant that is entirely consumed during a reaction, limiting the amount of product that can be formed. To identify the limiting reagent, compare the amounts of each reactant and determine which will run out first based on the balanced equation.

The theoretical yield is the maximum possible product produced, while the actual yield is the measured product. Percent yield compares actual yield to theoretical yield.
