Topic: Stocks and Bonds

Stocks

Corporations may raise money for their expansion by issuing stocks. Stocks represent shares in the ownership of the company. There are two types of stocks:

  • Common Stock - Represents basic ownership in a company.
  • Preferred Stock - Gives holders priority over common stockholders in receiving dividends.

Both types of stockholders receive dividends (a share of earnings). However, dividends are paid first to preferred shareholders. Stocks can be bought or sold at their current price, called the market value. Upon purchasing shares, the investor receives a certificate with details such as the corporation name, owner's name, number of shares, and par value per share.

Bonds

Bonds are interest-bearing securities that promise to pay a certain amount of money at a specified maturity date as outlined in the bond certificate. Bondholders are lenders to the issuing institution, which could be a government or a private company.

Unlike stockholders, bondholders do not have voting rights at annual meetings, but they are first in line to claim the institutional earnings. At maturity, bondholders receive the face value of the bond along with coupon payments (interest), which are typically made semi-annually, depending on the coupon rate stated in the bond certificate.

Definition of Terms

  • Stocks - Shares in the ownership of a company.
  • Dividend - A share in the company’s profit paid to stockholders.
  • Dividend Per Share - The ratio of dividends to the number of shares.
  • Stock Market - A place where stocks can be bought or sold, such as the Philippine Stock Exchange (PSE).
  • Market Value - The current price of a stock at which it can be sold.
  • Stock Yield Ratio - The ratio of the annual dividend per share to the market value per share.
  • Par Value - The per-share amount stated on the company certificate, fixed over time.
  • Zero-coupon Bond - A bond that does not pay periodic coupons and is issued at a discount.

Example: Investment in Stocks

Sir Abulencia and four of his co-teachers decided to invest different amounts to start a company. Their investments are as follows:

  • Ernie: ₱300,000
  • Sibling 1: ₱250,000
  • Sibling 2: ₱100,000
  • Sibling 3: ₱200,000
  • Sibling 4: ₱50,000

a. Ownership Percentages

To calculate the ownership percentage, divide each contribution by the total business value of ₱900,000.

Member Investment (₱) Ownership (%)
Ernie ₱300,000 33.33%
Sibling 1 ₱250,000 27.78%
Sibling 2 ₱100,000 11.11%
Sibling 3 ₱200,000 22.22%
Sibling 4 ₱50,000 5.56%

b. Cost per Share

The total business value is ₱900,000. If the business is split into 10,000 shares, the cost per share is:

₱900,000 ÷ 10,000 = ₱90

c. Number of Shares per Investor

To determine the number of shares each investor will have, multiply the ownership percentage by the total number of shares:

Member Shares
Ernie 3,333
Sibling 1 2,778
Sibling 2 1,111
Sibling 3 2,222
Sibling 4 556

Learn About It!

  • Stock Market - A marketplace for issuing and trading shares of stock through exchanges or brokers.
  • Stock Appreciation - The increase in the value of a stock from its original purchase price.
  • Bond Market - A financial market where debt securities are issued and traded.
  • Coupon - Interest payments received by bondholders, usually semi-annually.
  • Tenor or Term of a Bond - The fixed period of time until the bond is redeemable.
  • Fair Price of a Bond - The present value of all cash inflows expected from the bond.
  • Par Value or Face Value - The amount payable on a bond’s maturity date.
  • Required Yield - The return a bond must offer for an investment to be worthwhile.
  • Stock Yield Ratio - The ratio of annual dividend to the current market value per share of the stock.
