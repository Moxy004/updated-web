Topic: Binomial and Poisson Distribution

The Binomial Distribution

If a group of patients is given a new drug for the relief of a particular condition, then the proportion (p) being successfully treated can be regarded as estimating the population treatment success rate.

The sample proportion, p = r/n, is analogous to the sample mean. Binary data (0 for failure, 1 for success) follow the binomial distribution if the population response rate does not change.

Binomial Probability Formula

P(r responses out of n) = [n! / (r! * (n-r)!)] * π^r * (1-π)^(n-r)

Here, n! (read as "n factorial") represents the product of all positive integers up to n, and π represents the success probability.

The Poisson Distribution

The Poisson distribution describes discrete count data, where the population size (n) is large, the probability of an individual event (π) is small, but the expected number of events (λ = n * π) is moderate.

Poisson Probability Formula

P(r responses) = (λ^r * e^(-λ)) / r!

Where e is the exponential constant (approximately 2.718), and λ is the average rate of occurrence.

Other Distributions

t-distribution

Used for small sample sizes (n < 30), the t-distribution resembles the Normal distribution but has heavier tails. It provides better estimates for small datasets.

Chi-squared distribution

This right-skewed distribution is used in chi-squared tests to assess deviations between observed and expected frequencies or test the independence of categorical variables.
